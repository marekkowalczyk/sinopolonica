## Axioms

The system rests on five axioms. These are non-negotiable. Every
principle, rule, and example must be traceable to one or more axioms.

**A1 — Semantic Saturation.** Every morpheme that carries semantic content
receives a Chinese character. No exceptions. If you can ask "what does
this morpheme *mean*?", it gets a character. If you can only ask "what
grammatical function does this morpheme *perform*?", it stays in Latin
script.

**A2 — Etymological Exposure.** Fossilized forms are cracked open to
reveal their internal structure. The system represents morphemes as they
*were*, not as modern speakers perceive them. Historical opacity becomes
visual transparency.

**A3 — Register Through Script.** Common kanji (typically Japanese
simplified forms) signal everyday, native Slavic vocabulary. Rare
Classical Chinese characters signal learned, Latinate, or Greek
vocabulary. The character itself is the register marker — no other
annotation is needed.

**A4 — Suppletive Absorption.** Irregular stem alternations are hidden
beneath stable characters. The character is a semantic constant; the
reader infers pronunciation from context and from the Latin-script
inflectional ending.

**A5 — Minimal Latin Residue.** Latin script handles only material with
no independent semantic content: inflectional endings, proper nouns, and
numerals.

## Principles

Principles are derived from the axioms. They are numbered P*nn* for
stable cross-reference. When principles conflict, lower-numbered
principles take precedence unless explicitly overridden.

### Character assignment

**P01 — Stem test (from A1).** For any Polish word, identify all
semantic morphemes. Each one that passes the "what does it mean?" test
receives a character. Apply recursively: compound stems, derivational
suffixes, and prefixes must each be tested independently.

**P02 — Prefix integrity (from A1, A2).** Polish verbal prefixes
(prze-, wy-, do-, od-, na-, pod-, za-, w-) carry spatial or aspectual
meaning. Each receives the same character as its corresponding
preposition where one exists. A prefix that is purely aspectual with no
surviving spatial meaning (e.g., distributive *po-*) may remain in Latin
script.

**P03 — Derivational suffix test (from A1).** Productive derivational
suffixes that create new word categories are semantic, not inflectional.
They receive characters. Key examples: -arnia (place of X), -arz (agent),
-ość (abstract quality), -nik (agent/instrument), -stwo (domain/state).
See `07-derivation.md` for the full inventory.

**P04 — Inflectional residue (from A5).** The following, and only the
following, categories remain in Latin script: case endings, verb
person/tense/gender suffixes, comparative and superlative suffixes
(-iejszy, -iejsza), diminutive/augmentative suffixes (-ek, -eczek,
-isko), and the indefinite suffix -ś.

**P05 — No Latin stems (from A1).** If a morpheme originates from Latin
or Greek but functions as a semantic stem in Polish, it receives a
character from the learned register. The suffix *-graf* is not a Polish
ending — it is a Greek root meaning "writer/recorder" and receives a
character (書). The suffix *-ia* on *kardiomiopatia* is a Polish
grammatical adaptation and stays in Latin script.

### Character selection

**P06 — Single-character preference (from A1).** Classical Chinese
single-character vocabulary is the primary source. Modern Chinese
two-character compounds (熟語) are avoided. If Classical Chinese offers a
single character for a concept, use it. Resort to compounds only when no
single character exists.

**P07 — Register assignment (from A3).** Native Slavic stems receive
common, visually simple characters (typically shared with Japanese jōyō
kanji). Latinate/Greek borrowings receive rarer Classical Chinese
characters. When a concept has both a Slavic and a Latinate expression
(e.g., serce/kardio-), each gets its own character from the appropriate
register.

**P08 — Japanese simplification permitted (from A3).** When a Japanese
simplified (shinjitai) form exists for the same meaning as a complex
Classical form, the simplified form is the default for everyday register.
The Classical form is reserved for learned register or may coexist as a
high-register variant.

### Reading and pronunciation

**P09 — Multi-reading absorption (from A4).** A single character absorbs
all suppletive stem variants of a word. The verb 歩 (go/walk) absorbs
id-, szed-, sz-, chodz-. The pronoun 吾 (I) absorbs ja-, mn-, mną-. The
reader infers the correct stem from the inflectional ending and context.

**P10 — Ending-driven disambiguation (from A4, A5).** The Latin-script
ending is sufficient to determine case, gender, number, person, tense,
and mood. Redundant stem material is not written. 彼o (= jego/go) rather
than 彼ego, because the -o ending alone marks GEN/ACC.

### Fossilized forms

**P11 — Decomposition mandate (from A2).** Polish words whose internal
morpheme boundaries have been obscured by sound change must be decomposed
into their historical components. *Nikt* is written 不誰 (not + who).
*Wziąć* is written wz取ąć (perfective prefix + seize). *Swój* is
written 己ój (reflexive + possessive ending).

**P12 — Historical layering (from A2).** Where a morpheme has changed
meaning over centuries, the system may preserve traces of both the
original and the modern meaning. The conditional *-by-* was originally an
aorist of "to be" (存); it now marks hypotheticality (假). Both
characters are valid — 存 in archaizing texts, 假 as the modern standard.
The system acknowledges historical strata rather than flattening them.

### Scope boundaries

**P13 — Proper nouns in Latin.** All proper nouns (personal names, place
names, brand names) remain in Latin script. Polish Latin script *is* the
phonetic script of this system — there is no need for a katakana
equivalent.

**P14 — Three-tier numerals.** Arabic numerals for everyday use, Roman
numerals for formal contexts (as in current Polish), Chinese numeral
characters for ceremonial or calligraphic contexts.

**P15 — Western orthographic conventions.** Punctuation, writing
direction (left to right, horizontal), and paragraph structure follow
standard Polish conventions. No CJK punctuation or vertical writing is
imported.

**P16 — Compound enrichment option.** A written compound may carry more
semantic information than the spoken word. A silent classifier (e.g., 機
= machine) may be added to a compound like 己行 (samochód = self-go) to
produce 己行機 (self-go-machine) without changing pronunciation. This is a
script-level enrichment, not a neologism in speech. It is optional and
must be explicitly marked in the lexicon.

### Negation and interrogatives

**P17 — Negation is semantic (from A1).** The negator *nie* is always
written 不. It is never written in Latin script.

**P18 — Interrogative decomposition (from A2, A11).** Polish negative
pronouns (nikt, nic, nigdzie, nigdy), indefinite pronouns (ktoś, coś,
gdzieś, kiedyś), and universal pronouns (wszędzie, zawsze) are
decomposed into their component morphemes: negator/quantifier +
interrogative root (+ indefinite suffix where applicable).
